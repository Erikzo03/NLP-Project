\begin{abstract}
This project studies how to improve Danish Named Entity Recognition (NER) by transferring knowledge from English with BERT-based models. Because Danish has less labeled data, we compare zero-shot transfer, transfer plus Danish fine-tuning, and a Danish-only baseline. We use mBERT and XLM-R for transfer and include an English BERT baseline. We also test whether English and Danish being related Indo-European languages gives a practical transfer advantage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Background}
NER needs labeled data, which is abundant for English but limited for Danish. This makes Danish NER harder to train with only supervised data. A practical solution is transfer learning: train on English first, then adapt to Danish.

Earlier work supports this idea. \citet{plank2020danish} reported English-to-Danish transfer with zero-shot F1 around 58--61\%, and above 70\% when small Danish labeled sets were added. \citet{fu2023heterogeneous} found transfer is usually stronger for related languages, and \citet{sohn2024zeroshot} showed that surface similarity also helps. Since English and Danish are related Indo-European languages, this pair is a strong test case.

\section{Research Questions}
Our main question is: \textit{How much can English-to-Danish transfer improve Danish NER with multilingual BERT-based models?} We focus on three sub-questions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item How well do simple monolingual English and Danish BERT baselines perform?
  \item How much performance do we lose in zero-shot English-to-Danish transfer compared with supervised Danish training?
  \item Is two-step training (first English, then Danish) better than both zero-shot transfer and Danish-only training?
\end{itemize}
We also test whether English and Danish being related languages gives a measurable transfer advantage, in line with the findings of \citet{fu2023heterogeneous}.

\section{Methodology}
We use token classification with transformer encoders in four setups: (1) an English baseline with \texttt{bert-base-cased}, (2) zero-shot transfer to Danish with mBERT and XLM-R trained on English, (3) transfer fine-tuning where those models are further trained on Danish, and (4) a Danish-only baseline. This design lets us separate three effects: pure transfer, transfer plus target-language adaptation, and fully local Danish training.

To study data efficiency, we also fine-tune with 10\%, 25\%, and 50\% of Danish training data.

We report entity-level F1, precision, and recall, and we include per-label and error analysis (boundary mistakes, label confusion, and tokenization issues). For reproducibility, we keep random seeds fixed and log all hyperparameters.

\section{Expected Outcome}
We expect zero-shot transfer to be below fully supervised Danish training but above a weak baseline. We expect transfer fine-tuning to give the best Danish results, especially with limited Danish data.
