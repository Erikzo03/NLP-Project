\section{Related Work}

Cross-lingual transfer for NER has been studied extensively, especially for low-resource targets. \citet{plank2020danish} showed that English-to-Danish transfer can already be useful in zero-shot settings and improves further with small amounts of Danish supervision. More generally, \citet{fu2023heterogeneous} found that source-target similarity matters, especially when the languages share structural properties.

Multilingual encoders are the main enabler of this transfer. BERT \citep{devlin2019bert} introduced the transformer pretraining recipe that became the foundation for many multilingual systems. XLM-R \citep{conneau2020xlmr} improved on this by scaling data and coverage, which is why it often gives stronger cross-lingual results than mBERT. For Danish NER, this matters because transfer quality is limited by how well the encoder can align source and target representations.

Linguistic distance has also been used to explain transfer behavior. Typological databases such as URIEL+ \citep{khan2025urielplus} and language-distance collections such as DistALS \citep{goot2025distals} provide structured ways to compare languages. \citet{sohn2024zeroshot} further showed that even surface-level language similarity can help in zero-shot NER transfer. Our work extends this line by comparing English and Norwegian directly for Danish and by connecting the measured transfer gap to the expected linguistic-distance gap.

Finally, the dataset setting follows the standard multilingual NER benchmark tradition. WikiANN \citep{mayhew2024universal} gives a consistent IOB2 annotation scheme across languages, which is ideal for transfer experiments because it removes dataset-format confounds.
