\label{tab:multiseed}

Three observations stand out. First, seed 42 made Norwegian XLM-R look clearly better on dev (86.49 vs.\ 85.52) — until you average across seeds, at which point English XLM-R pulls slightly ahead (85.87 vs.\ 85.45). That reversal encapsulates the cautionary message: single-seed development comparisons can mislead. Second, on the held-out test set Norwegian leads for both encoders: +1.86 F1 for XLM-R ($p=0.10$) and +3.36 F1 for mBERT ($p=0.046$). Third, the mBERT result is the more reliable of the two — a larger margin, lower variance, and conventional significance despite the limited number of usable seed pairs.

\subsection{Per-entity-type F1}

Table~\ref{tab:entity_type} shows per-type F1 for key systems on the Danish development set.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\resizebox{\linewidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
System & PER & LOC & ORG \\
\midrule
Danish BERT baseline        & 91.6 & 91.3 & 82.4 \\
Danish XLM-R baseline       & 93.7 & 89.3 & 79.6 \\
EN→DA XLM-R zero-shot       & 89.8 & 86.5 & 77.3 \\
NO→DA XLM-R zero-shot       & 91.0 & 90.0 & 74.9 \\
EN→DA mBERT + DA FT         & 91.0 & 92.7 & 83.5 \\
NO→DA mBERT + DA FT         & 89.6 & 90.5 & 81.7 \\
NO→DA XLM-R + DA FT         & 93.1 & 90.6 & 78.8 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}%
}
\caption{Per-entity-type strict F1 (\%) on Danish dev. MISC is excluded: those annotations appear only in a secondary label column not read by the training and evaluation code, so MISC F1 is 0.0 for all systems by construction (see Section~\ref{sec:analysis}).}
\label{tab:entity_type}
\end{table}

ORG is the most variable category across systems, swinging from 74.9 to 83.5 — a spread that far exceeds what either PER or LOC shows. The EN→DA mBERT + DA FT model records the highest ORG score (83.5), which contributes to its strong overall ranking in Table~\ref{tab:dev_results}. LOC shows a notable zero-shot advantage for Norwegian XLM-R (90.0 vs.\ 86.5 for English), plausibly because place names share more lexical surface form between Norwegian and Danish. PER is the most stable category across all systems (89.6--93.7), consistent with the broader finding that person names transfer more reliably across languages.
