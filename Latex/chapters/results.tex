\subsection{Statistical significance of the EN vs NO gap}
\label{sec:results}

Table~\ref{tab:bootstrap} reports the result of a paired approximate randomisation test (10\,000 bootstrap iterations) on the NO→DA XLM-R vs EN→DA XLM-R zero-shot comparison.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccr}
\toprule
Split & F1 (NO) & F1 (EN) & $\Delta$ & $p$ \\
\midrule
Dev  & 86.49 & 85.52 & +0.97 & 0.575 \\
Test & 82.46 & 81.86 & +0.60 & 0.742 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Paired bootstrap significance test for NO→DA vs EN→DA XLM-R zero-shot. The gap is directionally consistent but not statistically significant at any conventional threshold ($p \gg 0.05$).}
\label{tab:bootstrap}
\end{table}

The advantage of Norwegian is directionally consistent across both splits but not statistically significant ($p=0.58$ dev, $p=0.74$ test). This means the 1.0 F1 gap on the development set falls within normal run-to-run variability and cannot be attributed to linguistic distance alone. Multi-seed replication is needed to confirm whether the directional advantage is reliable. We report and discuss the directional pattern while acknowledging this limitation.

ORG is the hardest category, with the most variation across systems (74.9--83.5). NO→DA XLM-R zero-shot leads on LOC (90.0 vs 86.5 for EN) --- a gap that is consistent with Norwegian having more lexical overlap with Danish for place names. PER is the most consistent category across systems (89.6--93.7).
