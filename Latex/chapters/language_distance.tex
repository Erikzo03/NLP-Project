\section{Linguistic Distance Analysis}

\subsection{Motivation}

The proposal argued that Norwegian should transfer better than English because it is closer to Danish. This report tests that hypothesis directly. Norwegian and Danish are both North Germanic, while English is Germanic but much more distant from Danish in history and structure. If linguistic distance matters, Norwegian should provide a stronger transfer signal.

\subsection{Computation}

We use typological feature vectors derived from WALS-style language descriptions and compute cosine distance over aligned features. Missing values are excluded from the comparison. In formula form, the distance between languages $L_1$ and $L_2$ is
\[
d(L_1, L_2) = 1 - \frac{\mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_2}{\|\mathbf{v}_1\|\,\|\mathbf{v}_2\|}.
\]

The purpose of this step is not to claim a perfect theory of similarity, but to get a concrete, measurable proxy that can be compared against observed NER transfer performance.

The distance results support the transfer hypothesis. Norwegian is considerably closer to Danish than English, and the observed zero-shot results follow the same pattern. That is the central evidence for the claim that source language choice is not arbitrary: it should be guided by measured similarity whenever possible.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The distance analysis does not prove causality by itself, but it does strengthen the empirical story. When a closer source language is also the better transfer source, the simplest explanation is that the multilingual encoder can reuse more of the learned structure for the target language.
